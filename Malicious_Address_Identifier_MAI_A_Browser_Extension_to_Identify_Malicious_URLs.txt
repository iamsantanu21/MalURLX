Abstract—Many websites have emerged since the internet was
introduced. Every day, people visit a variety of websites, and
they unknowingly click on malicious URLs because they simply
neglect to check the URLs they visit. Clicking on a malicious URL
has significant consequences. People might become victims of a
phishing attack, which aims to steal people’s money or identities
by tricking them into disclosing their personal data such as
passwords, credit card numbers, and bank account numbers on
websites that portray themselves as legitimate. They might also
have malware automatically installed on their computer devices,
which can harm and destroy their systems. Therefore, if people
don’t know whether a URL is malicious or not, something more
dangerous might happen. In this paper, we present a browser
extension that employs a two-layer model to identify malicious
URLs on the web using Bi-LSTM and CNN algorithms. This
extension not only identifies malicious URLs but also prevents
them from being visited. Furthermore, it blocks ads so that users
do not click on malicious URLs through ads.
Index Terms —Browser Extension, Bi-LSTM, CNN, Deep

I. I NTRODUCTION
Web browsers are now widely used by a significant number
of people to visit numerous websites on the Internet. In a
single day, the Google Search Engine scans around 20 billion
URLs, and according to a study, one-third of the URLs in use
today are malicious [1]. The purpose behind generating these
malicious URLs is often terrible, such as advancing a political
agenda, stealing confidential information from businesses, or
making quick money. Hackers frequently use popular search
terms and viral videos to disseminate malicious software or
links to phishing websites that operate maliciously on users’
systems or trick them into revealing their personal data online
[2].
Trojan horses, ransomware, worms, and spyware are
examples of malicious software that targets people’s
computers. These viruses ultimately aim to damage the
user’s device, access personal information, and make money.
Moreover, they could damage the network of the business,
which would cost money. Although the objectives and
methods used vary, the common purpose is to lure unknowing
people to these malicious sites, which can be reached by
links in emails, search engine results, or website redirection
[3].
To identify malicious URLs, passive detection tech-
niques often use lexical and host-based characteristics.
Lexical characteristics are those that differ in appear-
ance from actual URLs. For instance, the malicious URL
www.paypal.com.pay.net has more ”.” than the legitimate URL
www.paypal.com [4]. MAI uses a two-layer model to identify
malicious URLs on the internet using Bi-LSTM and CNN
algorithms that, in addition to identifying malicious URLs,
prohibit their access and filter ads to prevent users from
clicking on them via ads.

III. R ELATED WORK
The following approaches were identified in the literature
after evaluating a number of research papers:
A. Comparative Study:
In order to compare the effectiveness of multiple well-
known machine learning classifiers, the URLs were catego-
rized as benign or dangerous as part of a binary classification
problem. A public dataset of 450,000 URLs from Kaggle was
used to train the model, out of which 23.2% (104,438) were
malicious URLs and the rest were benign URLs. Machine
learning classification algorithms such as Logistic Regression,
Stochastic Gradient Descent, Random Forest, Support Vector
Machine (SVM), Naive Bayes, K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN),
and Decision Tree were used, and on the OpenPhish website,
the most accurate classifier was used to find malicious URLs.
Therefore, it turns out that the Random Forest achieves the
maximum accuracy and FI score [5].
B. Multi-Layer Model:
Using a multi-layer model, the URLs were categorized as
malicious or normal. The model primarily comprises four-
layer classifiers, which are referred to as filters in this model.
Therefore, the model comprises a four-layer filter composition.
The filter can quickly determine whether a URL is malicious
or not by training each layer’s threshold as it approaches
the threshold. If not, the filter hands the URL over to the
next layer. Machine learning classifiers such as Naive Bayes,
Decision Tree, and SVM were used to train the model, using
a dataset of 20,000 samples, a dataset of 10,000 malicious
URLs from the malicious website lab, and a dataset of 10,000
normal URLs from the first category directory [6].
C. Attention Based BiLSTM Model:
An AB-BiLSTM model based on deep learning was used to
identify malicious URLs. The model automatically picks up
new features, and while analyzing URL sequences, it employs
an attention mechanism to capture anomalous segments. First,
the URLs were preprocessed and turned into word vectors
using pre-trained Word2Vec. Next, BiLSTM was trained in
conjunction with an attention mechanism to extract features
from URL sequences and classify them. The labeled URL
dataset of 453,340 URLs, of which 344,821 are benign URLs
from DMOZ and 108,519 are malicious URLs from VirusTotal
and PhishTank, was used to test the model The experimental
findings demonstrate that the suggested model is capable of
achieving an accuracy rate of 98.06% and an F1 score of
95.22% [7].
D. K-Means Clustering:
Malicious URLs were grouped using K-Means clustering
utilizing a dataset obtained from an open source threat intel-
ligence feed framework comprising more than 11,000 mali-
cious URLs. The dataset of malicious URLs contains hidden
structures that were found using the unsupervised learning
technique. Clustering of URL keywords reveals important
information regarding malicious URLs. The clusters generated
using the method have a silhouette coefficient of 0.383. Finally,
in order to determine the percentage of malicious keywords
included in a specified URL, a probabilistic scoring model was
created. The model correctly recognised that more than 80%
of the URLs in a test sample were malicious after analyzing
more than 72,000 malicious keywords [8].
E. Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) Model:
Phishing URLs were identified based on the URL content.
Online training and online testing make up the two compo-
nents of the neural network-based approach to identifying ma-
licious URLs. In order to access the page content, the simple
features were first utilized for preliminary screening before
being applied to the URLs that couldn’t be distinguished by
them. The approach based on CNN is then used to identify
malicious URLs based on the content of the page. Another
key element in identifying malicious websites is the webpage
image. Therefore, a novel CNN-based method was also created
to detect malicious URLs through the analysis of website
images [9].

the URL of the active tab and forbids people from accessing
it if it is found malicious. MAI can be enabled and disabled as

The MAI model has two layers, with the first layer
recognising the malicious URL using the conventional
blacklisting method. If the URL is not present in the array
of URLs that have been blacklisted, the model moves on
to the next layer after searching the array. To identify the
malicious URLs, the second layer employs Bi-LSTM and
CNN algorithms. Finally, if the URL is malicious, it prevents
anyone from viewing it.
The MAI model’s two layers are described in detail below:
A. Layer-1: URL Filtering
The conventional blacklisting technique is used in the first
layer of the MAI model to find malicious URLs online. It looks
for the URL in the list of URLs that have been blacklisted, and
if it finds the URL there, it prevents people from accessing it.
Otherwise, it passes the URL on to the next layer for additional
scrutiny, as shown in Fig. 2.
B. Layer-2: Bi-LSTM + CNN
The skip-gram pre-training model provides the initial em-
bedding representation of the URLs for the second layer of
the MAI model. Then, context dependency is extracted using
Bi-LSTM to further understand the deep representation of
URLs. Furthermore, CNNs are used to extract the local n-
gram characteristics [10], as shown in Fig. 3.

IMPLEMENTATION
Fig. 4 illustrates the flow of the second layer of the MAI,
and the subsequent explanation of each layer’s functioning
and the actions taken by it is described below:
a) InputLayer: Artificial input neurons make up this
layer, which brings in the first batch of information that
artificial neurons in later levels will process.
b) Embedding: Dense vectors are used to represent
words, with each vector representing the word’s projection
into a continuous vector space.
c) Bidirectional(LSTM): It is used to better understand
the deep representation of URLs, and context dependency is
extracted. It significantly increases the quantity of information
that is available to the network, which enhances the context
that the algorithm can use.

SOCIAL IMPACT
A lot of people who neglect to double-check the URLs they
visit benefit from MAI because it stops them from viewing
malicious URLs on the internet. Now, users can browse the
web without bothering about checking the URL.
The URL may often be hyperlinked to an image or other
piece of media, making it invisible to people. Such hyperlinks
can be found all over the Internet. Every day, people who use
the internet for browsing come across numerous hyperlinked
pieces of media. MAI also keeps an eye on redirection and
prevents people from falling for the clickbait on the internet.
Clickbait attracts people by displaying content that they are
interested in or by using fake news to advance an agenda [11].
Furthermore, websites like these are sometimes used for terror-
ist activities to transfer information in various languages [12]
[13] [14]. MAI protects people from visiting such websites.
APPLICATIONS
The applications of MAI include:
1) World Wide Web: The World Wide Web (WWW) is an
interconnected network fueled by the collection of links found
throughout the Internet. The Internet link may be malicious
and deceive unknowing people. MAI extension will protect
such people from clicking malicious links.
2) Social Media: Social media is widely used today, and
many rumors are disseminated to mislead people. People are
drawn to these rumors and may click on them, which may
lead to malicious websites. Where they could disclose their
personal information [15]. The MAI model can be integrated
with social media to check for such links.
